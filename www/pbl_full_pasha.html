Classification of Elements & Periodicity
Periodic Table Structure
  • Modern Periodic Law: Properties are periodic functions of atomic number
  • Periods (1–7): Horizontal rows → indicate number of shells
  • Groups (1–18): Vertical columns → indicate valence electrons (main group)
  • 118 elements currently known; 7 periods, 18 groups
Periodic Trends — Atomic & Ionic Size
  • Atomic Radius: Decreases across period (↑ nuclear charge), increases down group (↑ shells)
  • Cation: Smaller than neutral atom (fewer electrons, same/more nuclear charge)
  • Anion: Larger than neutral atom (more electrons, same nuclear charge)
  • Isoelectronic species: size decreases with increasing atomic number
Ionization Enthalpy (IE)
  • Energy required to remove an electron from gaseous atom
  • Increases across period, decreases down group
  • Exception: Be > B (2s fully filled vs 2p); N > O (half-filled 2p stable)
  • IE₁ < IE₂ < IE₃ … (successive IEs increase sharply after noble gas config)
Electron Gain Enthalpy & Electronegativity
  • EGE: Energy released when e⁻ added to neutral atom; more negative across period
  • Halogens: Highest negative EGE (Cl has highest, not F due to small size repulsion)
  • Noble gases: Positive EGE (stable config, electron not accepted easily)
  • Electronegativity: Increases across period, decreases down group
  • Order: F > O > N ~ Cl > Br > I > C
Metallic & Acid/Base Character of Oxides
  • Metallic character: Decreases across period, increases down group
  • Non-metallic character: Increases across period, decreases down group
  • Basic oxides: Group 1 & 2 metals (Na₂O, MgO)
  • Acidic oxides: Non-metals (SO₃, P₂O₅, Cl₂O₇)
  • Amphoteric oxides: Al₂O₃, ZnO, SnO (react with both acid & base)
  • Acidity of oxides increases across period (basic → neutral → acidic)
Chemical Bonding & Molecular Structure
Types of Bonds
  • Ionic Bond: Transfer of electrons; electrostatic attraction; favored by low IE, high EGE, high lattice enthalpy
  • Covalent Bond: Sharing of electrons; sigma (σ) and pi (π) bonds
  • Coordinate/Dative: Both electrons from one atom (e.g., BF₃ + NH₃)
  • Metallic Bond: Sea of electrons; explains conductivity and malleability
Hybridization & VSEPR
  • sp³: CH₄ (tetrahedral 109.5°), NH₃ (pyramidal), H₂O (bent)
  • sp²: BF₃ (trigonal planar 120°), C₂H₄
  • sp: BeCl₂ (linear 180°), C₂H₂
  • VSEPR: Lone pairs > bond pairs in repulsion → compress bond angles
  • Bond angle: CH₄ (109.5°) > NH₃ (107°) > H₂O (104.5°)
  • dsp² (square planar), sp³d (trigonal bipyramidal), sp³d² (octahedral)
Molecular Orbital Theory (MOT)
  • Atomic orbitals combine to form bonding (BMO) and antibonding (ABMO) MOs
  • Bond Order (BO) = ½(Nb − Na); higher BO = stronger, shorter bond
  • BO = 0 → molecule doesn't exist (e.g., He₂)
  • O₂: BO = 2, paramagnetic (2 unpaired e⁻ in π* orbitals)
  • N₂: BO = 3, diamagnetic, very stable
  • Unpaired e⁻ → paramagnetic; all paired → diamagnetic
Hydrogen Bonding & Intermolecular Forces
  • H-bond: Dipole–dipole interaction between H and electronegative atom (F, O, N)
  • Intermolecular H-bond: Between molecules (H₂O, HF, NH₃) → high BP
  • Intramolecular H-bond: Within molecule (o-nitrophenol) → lower BP than para
  • Strength: F–H···F > O–H···O > N–H···N
  • Van der Waals (weakest) → Dipole–dipole → H-bond → Covalent (strongest)
Octet Rule & Exceptions
  • Octet: atoms gain/lose/share electrons to achieve 8 valence electrons
  • Incomplete octet: BF₃ (6e⁻ around B), BeCl₂ (4e⁻ around Be)
  • Expanded octet: PCl₅ (10e⁻), SF₆ (12e⁻) — 3rd period & beyond (d-orbitals)
  • Odd-electron molecules: NO, NO₂ (odd number of electrons)
s-Block Elements
Group 1 — Alkali Metals (Li, Na, K, Rb, Cs, Fr)
  • Config: [Noble gas] ns¹; all have +1 oxidation state
  • Highly reactive; strong reducing agents; form ionic compounds
  • React with water: 2M + 2H₂O → 2MOH + H₂↑ (vigour: Li < Na < K < Rb < Cs)
  • Flame colours: Li (crimson red), Na (golden yellow), K (lilac/violet), Rb (red-violet), Cs (blue)
  • Li shows anomalous behaviour — diagonal relationship with Mg
  • Li forms Li₃N (nitride) directly; Na forms Na₂O₂ (peroxide); K, Rb, Cs form superoxides (MO₂)
Group 2 — Alkaline Earth Metals (Be, Mg, Ca, Sr, Ba, Ra)
  • Config: [Noble gas] ns²; +2 oxidation state
  • Less reactive than alkali metals; harder, higher melting points
  • Flame colours: Ca (brick red), Sr (crimson red), Ba (apple green)
  • Be anomalous: Forms covalent compounds; diagonal relationship with Al; BeO is amphoteric
  • Limestone: CaCO₃; Plaster of Paris: CaSO₄·½H₂O
  • Gypsum: CaSO₄·2H₂O (+ heat → POP)
  • Solubility of sulfates: BeSO₄ > MgSO₄ > CaSO₄ > SrSO₄ > BaSO₄ (↓)
  • Solubility of hydroxides: Be(OH)₂ < Mg(OH)₂ < Ca(OH)₂ < Sr(OH)₂ < Ba(OH)₂ (↑)
p-Block Elements
Group 13 — Boron Family
  • Config: ns²np¹; Boron is non-metal, rest metals
  • BH₃ dimerizes → B₂H₆ (diborane) with 3-centre 2-electron (banana) bonds
  • Borax: Na₂B₄O₇·10H₂O; Boric acid: H₃BO₃ (weak monobasic acid)
  • Aluminium is amphoteric (reacts with acid AND alkali)
  • Inert pair effect: Tl prefers +1 state
  • BF₃ is a Lewis acid (incomplete octet)
Group 14 — Carbon Family
  • C allotropes: Diamond (sp³, hardest), Graphite (sp², lubricant, conductor), Fullerene (C₆₀)
  • Catenation: Self-linking ability; strongest in C due to strong C–C bonds
  • Si, Ge: metalloids; Sn, Pb: metals
  • Silicones: Polymers with −(R₂SiO)ₙ− repeating units; water-repellent
  • CO₂ (acidic, linear) vs SiO₂ (covalent network solid)
  • Inert pair effect: Pb prefers +2 state
Group 15 — Nitrogen Family
  • N is gaseous; P, As, Sb, Bi are solids
  • N has no d-orbitals → max covalency 4; P can expand to 5 (PCl₅)
  • Allotropes of P: White (toxic, most reactive), Red, Black (least reactive)
  • NH₃: Reducing agent, basic, pyramidal (sp³)
  • HNO₃: Strong oxidizing acid; dilute → NO; conc. → NO₂
  • Nitrogen forms oxides: N₂O, NO, N₂O₃, NO₂, N₂O₅
Group 16 — Chalcogens (Oxygen Family)
  • O is gaseous; S, Se, Te, Po are solids
  • Allotropes of S: Rhombic (stable <96°C), Monoclinic (stable 96–119°C)
  • H₂SO₄: Strong acid, dehydrating agent, oxidizing agent; fuming = oleum (H₂S₂O₇)
  • Ozone (O₃): Bent, diamagnetic, strong oxidizing agent; decomposes at 250°C
  • SO₂: Acidic, reducing agent (acts as bleach), V-shaped (sp³)
Group 17 — Halogens
  • Highly reactive non-metals; strong oxidizing agents; −1 common OS
  • F is most electronegative (no d-orbitals → no expansion, no positive OS)
  • Acidity of HX: HF < HCl < HBr < HI (bond strength decreases)
  • Oxidizing power: F₂ > Cl₂ > Br₂ > I₂
  • Interhalogen compounds: XYₙ type (ClF, ClF₃, ClF₅, BrF₃, IF₇)
  • Cl₂ with NaOH (cold dilute) → NaCl + NaOCl; (hot conc.) → NaCl + NaClO₃
Group 18 — Noble Gases
  • Config: ns²np⁶ (He: 1s²); inert, monoatomic, very low BP
  • He: lightest noble gas; used in balloons, diving (with O₂)
  • Ar: most abundant noble gas in atmosphere
  • Xe compounds: XeF₂ (linear), XeF₄ (square planar), XeF₆ (distorted octahedral), XeO₃
  • Kr, Rn also form some fluorides
d- & f-Block Elements
Transition Elements (d-Block, Groups 3–12)
  • Config: (n−1)d¹⁻¹⁰ ns¹⁻²
  • Variable oxidation states (due to d-electrons)
  • High melting/boiling points; hard; good conductors
  • Colored ions: d–d transitions absorb visible light
  • Paramagnetic due to unpaired d-electrons
  • Act as catalysts (e.g., Fe in Haber, V₂O₅ in contact process)
  • Form interstitial compounds (e.g., steel)
  • Zn, Cd, Hg: NOT true transition elements (d¹⁰ in all common oxidation states)
Important d-Block Compounds
  • KMnO₄: Purple; strong oxidizing agent; acidic medium → Mn²⁺ (colourless); neutral → MnO₂; alkaline → MnO₄²⁻
  • K₂Cr₂O₇: Orange; strong oxidizing agent; in acid: Cr₂O₇²⁻ + 14H⁺ + 6e⁻ → 2Cr³⁺ + 7H₂O
  • KMnO₄ can be used in acidic, neutral, or alkaline medium; K₂Cr₂O₇ only acidic
  • Cr is +6 in dichromate; Mn is +7 in permanganate
Lanthanoids (4f, Ce–Lu)
  • Config: (n−2)f¹⁻¹⁴ (n−1)d⁰⁻¹ ns²
  • Most common OS: +3
  • Lanthanoid Contraction: Steady decrease in atomic/ionic radii across lanthanoids due to poor shielding by 4f electrons
  • Causes: radii of 2nd and 3rd row transition elements to be nearly equal
  • Misch metal (La, Ce alloy): used in cigarette lighters
Actinoids (5f, Th–Lr)
  • Config: (n−2)f¹⁻¹⁴ (n−1)d⁰⁻¹ ns²
  • Exhibit variable oxidation states more than lanthanoids
  • All radioactive; most are man-made (synthetic)
  • Actinoid contraction: More pronounced than lanthanoid contraction (more effective shielding in 5f)
  • Only Th, Pa, U occur naturally in appreciable amounts
Coordination Compounds
Key Definitions
  • Coordination compound: Central metal atom/ion bonded to ligands
  • Ligand: Lewis base (electron pair donor) — monodentate, bidentate, polydentate
  • Coordination Number (CN): Number of donor atoms directly bonded to metal
  • Werner's Theory: Primary valency (ionizable = charge) & Secondary valency (CN = non-ionizable)
  • Examples of ligands: NH₃, H₂O, Cl⁻, CN⁻, en (bidentate), EDTA (hexadentate)
IUPAC Nomenclature
  • Name ligands first (alphabetical), then metal with OS in Roman numerals
  • Anionic ligands: -o suffix (Cl⁻ → chlorido, CN⁻ → cyanido, OH⁻ → hydroxido)
  • Neutral ligands: as-is (NH₃ → ammine, H₂O → aqua, CO → carbonyl)
  • If complex is anionic, metal gets -ate suffix + Latin name (Fe → ferrate, Cu → cuprate)
  • Example: [Co(NH₃)₄Cl₂]Cl → Tetraamminedichloridocobalt(III) chloride
Isomerism in Coordination Compounds
  • Ionization: [Co(NH₃)₅Cl]SO₄ vs [Co(NH₃)₅SO₄]Cl
  • Hydrate: [Cr(H₂O)₆]Cl₃ vs [Cr(H₂O)₅Cl]Cl₂·H₂O
  • Linkage: –NO₂ (N-bonded) vs –ONO (O-bonded); –SCN vs –NCS
  • Geometric (cis/trans): MA₂B₂ square planar & MA₄B₂ octahedral
  • Optical (enantiomers): [Co(en)₃]³⁺ — common in octahedral with bidentate ligands
  • Coordination isomerism: Exchange of ligands between cation and anion complexes
Crystal Field Theory (CFT)
  • Ligands as point charges split d-orbitals into two sets
  • Octahedral: t₂g (lower, dxy, dyz, dxz) and eg (higher, dx²-y², dz²); splitting = Δo
  • Tetrahedral: e (lower) and t₂ (higher); Δt ≈ 4/9 Δo (weaker)
  • Strong field ligands: CO > CN⁻ > NO₂⁻ > en > NH₃ → low spin (pair first)
  • Weak field ligands: I⁻ < Br⁻ < Cl⁻ < F⁻ < OH⁻ < H₂O → high spin
  • Color: d–d transition absorbs one color, transmits complementary color
  • Spectrochemical series: CO > CN⁻ > NO₂⁻ > en > NH₃ > H₂O > ox²⁻ > OH⁻ > F⁻ > Cl⁻ > Br⁻ > I⁻
VBT in Coordination Compounds
  • Strong field ligands (CN⁻, CO, NH₃) → inner orbital complexes (d²sp³ for octahedral)
  • Weak field ligands (H₂O, Cl⁻) → outer orbital complexes (sp³d² for octahedral)
  • Inner orbital: lower spin (more paired), diamagnetic if all paired
  • [Fe(CN)₆]⁴⁻: d²sp³, diamagnetic (strong field); [Fe(H₂O)₆]²⁺: sp³d², paramagnetic (weak field)
Some Basic Concepts of Chemistry
Mole Concept
  • 1 mole = 6.022 × 10²³ particles (Avogadro's number)
  • 1 mole of any gas at STP = 22.4 L
  • Molar mass (g) = mass of 1 mole of substance
  • Limiting reagent: Reactant consumed first; determines max product
  • % yield = (actual yield / theoretical yield) × 100
Concentration Terms
  • Molarity (M): Moles solute / L solution; changes with temperature
  • Molality (m): Moles solute / kg solvent; independent of temperature
  • Mole fraction (x): Moles component / Total moles
  • Mass %: (Mass component / Total mass) × 100
  • Normality (N): Equivalents / L; N = M × n-factor
Stoichiometry
  • Balanced equation gives molar ratios of reactants & products
  • Always identify limiting reagent before calculating product amount
  • Empirical formula: Simplest whole-number ratio of atoms
  • Molecular formula: Actual number of atoms; Mf = n × Ef
  • Law of conservation of mass: total mass reactants = total mass products
Structure of Atom
Bohr's Model
  • Electrons revolve in fixed circular orbits (shells) without radiating energy
  • Energy of nth orbit: Eₙ = −13.6/n² eV (for H-like atoms)
  • Radius: rₙ = 0.529n²/Z Å
  • Energy is emitted/absorbed when electron transitions between levels
  • Limitation: Cannot explain multi-electron atoms, Zeeman/Stark effect
Quantum Numbers
  • Principal (n): Shell, energy, size; n = 1, 2, 3…
  • Azimuthal (l): Subshell shape; l = 0 (s), 1 (p), 2 (d), 3 (f); l = 0 to n−1
  • Magnetic (mₗ): Orbital orientation; mₗ = −l to +l
  • Spin (mₛ): Electron spin; +½ or −½
  • Max electrons in shell = 2n²; in subshell: s(2), p(6), d(10), f(14)
Electronic Configuration Rules
  • Aufbau: Fill lowest energy orbitals first (1s, 2s, 2p, 3s, 3p, 4s, 3d…)
  • Hund's Rule: Maximize unpaired electrons in degenerate orbitals (same energy)
  • Pauli Exclusion: No two electrons can have same 4 quantum numbers
  • Exceptions: Cr = [Ar]3d⁵4s¹; Cu = [Ar]3d¹⁰4s¹ (extra stability of half-filled/fully-filled d)
Wave-Particle Duality & Uncertainty
  • de Broglie: λ = h/mv; every moving particle has wave character
  • Heisenberg Uncertainty: Δx · Δp ≥ h/4π; cannot know position & momentum exactly simultaneously
  • Photoelectric effect: light as photons, E = hν
  • Threshold frequency (ν₀): minimum frequency to eject electron
  • KE of ejected electron = hν − hν₀ = hν − W (W = work function)
Thermodynamics
Laws of Thermodynamics
  • First Law: ΔU = Q + W (energy conservation); ΔU = Q − PΔV (expansion)
  • Second Law: ΔS_total > 0 for spontaneous process; entropy of universe increases
  • Third Law: S → 0 as T → 0 K (perfect crystal has zero entropy at absolute zero)
  • Enthalpy: H = U + PV; at constant P: ΔH = ΔU + PΔV = ΔU + ΔngRT
Gibbs Free Energy & Spontaneity
  • G = H − TS
  • ΔG < 0: Spontaneous; ΔG = 0: Equilibrium; ΔG > 0: Non-spontaneous
  • ΔG° = −RT ln K (standard free energy & equilibrium constant)
  • ΔG = ΔG° + RT ln Q
  • At equilibrium: ΔG = 0 → ΔG° = −RT ln K
  • Exothermic + ΔS > 0: always spontaneous; Endothermic + ΔS < 0: never spontaneous
Hess's Law & Enthalpies
  • Hess's Law: ΔH is path-independent; total ΔH = sum of individual ΔH values
  • Lattice enthalpy: Energy to separate 1 mole of ionic crystal into gaseous ions
  • Bond enthalpy: Energy to break 1 mole of bonds; ΔH_rxn = ΣBE(broken) − ΣBE(formed)
  • Enthalpy of combustion: ΔH for complete combustion of 1 mole of substance
  • Born-Haber cycle: Calculates lattice enthalpy of ionic compounds
Heat Capacity & Work
  • Cp = (∂H/∂T)P; Cv = (∂U/∂T)V; Cp − Cv = R (for ideal gas)
  • Work done in expansion: W = −Pext·ΔV (expansion work against constant P)
  • Isothermal reversible: W = −nRT ln(V₂/V₁)
  • Adiabatic: Q = 0; ΔU = W
  • Isochoric: W = 0; ΔU = Qv
Equilibrium
Chemical Equilibrium
  • Law of Mass Action: Rate_forward = Rate_reverse at equilibrium
  • Kc = [C]ᶜ[D]ᵈ / [A]ᵃ[B]ᵇ (at equilibrium)
  • Kp = PCᶜ · PDᵈ / PAᵃ · PBᵇ
  • Relation: Kp = Kc(RT)^Δng where Δng = moles gaseous products − moles gaseous reactants
  • Large K: products favoured; Small K: reactants favoured
Le Chatelier's Principle
  • System shifts to relieve stress imposed on it
  • ↑ Concentration of reactant: Shifts right (→ products)
  • ↑ Pressure: Shifts toward fewer moles of gas
  • ↑ Temperature (exothermic rxn): Shifts left (← reactants); K decreases
  • ↑ Temperature (endothermic rxn): Shifts right; K increases
  • Catalyst: No shift in equilibrium; only speeds up attainment of equilibrium
  • Inert gas at constant V: No effect; at constant P: shifts to more moles of gas
Ionic Equilibrium
  • pH = −log[H⁺]; pOH = −log[OH⁻]; pH + pOH = 14 at 25°C
  • Kw = [H⁺][OH⁻] = 10⁻¹⁴ at 25°C
  • Buffer: Weak acid + its salt (or weak base + its salt); resists pH change
  • Henderson-Hasselbalch: pH = pKa + log([Salt]/[Acid])
  • Ksp: Product of ion concentrations at saturation; used to predict precipitation
  • Precipitation occurs when ion product > Ksp
  • Common ion effect: Ksp shifts equilibrium; reduces solubility
Redox Reactions
Oxidation & Reduction
  • Oxidation: Loss of electrons; increase in oxidation number
  • Reduction: Gain of electrons; decrease in oxidation number
  • Oxidizing agent: Causes oxidation; gets reduced (gains e⁻)
  • Reducing agent: Causes reduction; gets oxidized (loses e⁻)
  • OIL RIG: Oxidation Is Loss; Reduction Is Gain
Oxidation Number Rules
  • Pure element: OS = 0; Simple monoatomic ion: OS = charge
  • F always −1; O usually −2 (except OF₂: +2; peroxides: −1; superoxides: −½)
  • H: +1 with non-metals; −1 with metals (metal hydrides)
  • Sum of OS in neutral compound = 0; in ion = charge of ion
  • Mn in KMnO₄ = +7; Cr in K₂Cr₂O₇ = +6; S in H₂SO₄ = +6; N in HNO₃ = +5
Balancing Redox Reactions
  • Ion-electron method: Write half-reactions (oxidation & reduction); balance atoms, charge, then add
  • Oxidation number method: Track change in oxidation numbers; equalize increase & decrease
  • In acidic medium: add H₂O to balance O; add H⁺ to balance H
  • In basic medium: add OH⁻ to balance; then add H₂O for H
  • Disproportionation: single element simultaneously oxidized & reduced (e.g., Cl₂ + NaOH)
Solutions
Colligative Properties
  • Depend on number of solute particles, not identity
  • RLVP: (P° − Ps)/P° = xsolute (Raoult's Law)
  • Elevation in BP: ΔTb = Kb · m · i
  • Depression in FP: ΔTf = Kf · m · i
  • Osmotic Pressure: π = i · C · R · T
  • Kf (water) = 1.86 K·kg/mol; Kb (water) = 0.512 K·kg/mol
Van't Hoff Factor (i)
  • i = actual moles particles / theoretical moles particles
  • i = 1: Non-electrolytes (glucose, urea)
  • i > 1: Dissociation (NaCl → Na⁺ + Cl⁻, i = 2; K₂SO₄: i = 3)
  • i < 1: Association (acetic acid in benzene forms dimers)
  • α (degree of dissociation): i = 1 + (n−1)α where n = number of ions
Raoult's Law & Deviations
  • Ideal solution: Obeys Raoult's; ΔHmix = 0; ΔVmix = 0
  • +ve deviation: Pobs > Pideal; A–B forces weaker than A–A/B–B; ΔHmix > 0 (e.g., ethanol + water)
  • −ve deviation: Pobs < Pideal; A–B forces stronger; ΔHmix < 0 (e.g., HCl + water, H₂SO₄ + water)
  • Azeotropes: Constant boiling mixtures; cannot be separated by simple distillation
  • +ve deviation → minimum BP azeotrope; −ve deviation → maximum BP azeotrope
Electrochemistry
Galvanic vs Electrolytic Cells
  • Galvanic/Voltaic: Chemical energy → Electrical energy (spontaneous, ΔG < 0)
  • Electrolytic: Electrical energy → Chemical energy (non-spontaneous)
  • Anode: Oxidation (−ve in galvanic, +ve in electrolytic)
  • Cathode: Reduction (+ve in galvanic, −ve in electrolytic)
  • Salt bridge: Completes circuit; maintains electrical neutrality; contains KCl or KNO₃
Cell Potential & Nernst Equation
  • E°cell = E°cathode − E°anode (reduction potentials)
  • ΔG = −nFEcell; ΔG° = −nFE°cell
  • ΔG° = −RT ln K → E°cell = (RT/nF) ln K = (0.0592/n) log K at 298K
  • Nernst Equation: Ecell = E°cell − (0.0592/n) log Q at 298K
  • More +ve E°: stronger oxidizing agent; More −ve E°: stronger reducing agent
Faraday's Laws of Electrolysis
  • First Law: Mass deposited ∝ charge passed (W = ZQ = ZIt)
  • Second Law: For same charge, W₁/W₂ = E₁/E₂ (equivalent weights)
  • 1 Faraday = 96485 C ≈ 96500 C = charge of 1 mole of electrons
  • Quantity of charge: Q = I × t (coulombs)
Conductance
  • Conductance (G) = 1/R; unit: Siemens (S)
  • Conductivity (κ) = G · (l/A); unit: S m⁻¹
  • Molar Conductivity (Λm) = κ × 1000/M
  • Kohlrausch's Law: Λ°m = λ°₊ + λ°₋ (at infinite dilution)
  • Strong electrolytes: Λm increases linearly as concentration decreases
  • Weak electrolytes: Λm increases sharply at very low concentration
Chemical Kinetics
Rate Laws & Order
  • Rate of reaction: −d[A]/dt = k[A]ˣ[B]ʸ
  • Order: x + y (determined experimentally; can be fraction or zero)
  • Molecularity: Number of species in elementary step (always positive integer)
  • Rate constant k: units depend on order: (mol/L)^(1−n)/s
Integrated Rate Laws
  • Zero order: [A]t = [A]₀ − kt; t½ = [A]₀/2k; unit of k: mol/L·s
  • First order: ln[A]t = ln[A]₀ − kt; t½ = 0.693/k (independent of conc.); unit: s⁻¹
  • Second order: 1/[A]t = 1/[A]₀ + kt; t½ = 1/k[A]₀; unit: L/mol·s
  • Radioactive decay: always first order
Arrhenius Equation & Activation Energy
  • k = Ae^(−Ea/RT) or ln k = ln A − Ea/RT
  • Activation Energy (Ea): Minimum energy required for reaction to occur
  • Frequency Factor (A): Pre-exponential factor (collision frequency × steric factor)
  • ln(k₂/k₁) = (Ea/R)(1/T₁ − 1/T₂)
  • Catalyst: Provides alternate pathway with lower Ea; increases rate; does NOT change ΔG or K
  • Temperature: Every 10°C rise approximately doubles reaction rate
Collision Theory & Factors
  • Rate ↑ with: temperature↑, concentration↑, surface area↑, catalyst present
  • Effective collision: must have sufficient energy (≥ Ea) AND proper orientation
  • Threshold energy = Activation energy + average kinetic energy
  • Order vs molecularity: Molecularity only for elementary steps; order is experimental (may differ)
General Organic Chemistry
Electronic Effects
  • Inductive (+I): Electron-donating alkyl groups push σ-electrons
  • Inductive (−I): EWGs like −NO₂, −COOH pull σ-electrons
  • Resonance (+M): −OH, −NH₂ donate lone pair into ring/π
  • Resonance (−M): −NO₂, −CHO, −CN withdraw via π
  • Hyperconjugation: σ C−H electrons delocalize with adjacent p/π; more α-H = more stable
Acidity & Basicity
  • EWG increase acidity (stabilize conjugate base by −I/−M)
  • EDG decrease acidity; increase basicity
  • Resonance stabilization of conjugate base → stronger acid
  • Acidity order: RCOOH > PhOH > ROH > RC≡CH > Alkane
  • Basicity (gas): 3° > 2° > 1° > NH₃
  • Basicity (aq): 2° > 1° > 3° > NH₃ (methylamines)
  • Aromatic: Aliphatic amines > NH₃ > Aniline
Stability Orders
Carbocation (stabilized by +I, hyperconj, resonance)
3°> 2°> 1°> CH₃⁺
Carbanion (stabilized by −I, resonance)
CH₃⁻> 1°> 2°> 3°
Free Radical (hyperconj, resonance)
3°> 2°> 1°> CH₃•
Mechanisms — SN & E
  • SN1 2 steps · carbocation (RDS) → Nu attack · Racemization · 3°, weak Nu, polar protic
  • SN2 1 step · concerted · backside · Walden inversion · 1°, strong Nu, polar aprotic
  • E1 2 steps · carbocation → H⁺ loss · Zaitsev · 3°, weak base, polar protic
  • E2 1 step · anti-periplanar · Zaitsev · strong base
Isomerism at a Glance
  • Chain: Different carbon skeleton
  • Position: Different FG position
  • Functional: Different FG altogether
  • Metamers: Different alkyl groups on polyvalent atom
  • Tautomers: Dynamic equilibrium, proton transfer (keto-enol)
  • Geometrical: Restricted rotation at C=C or ring
  • Optical: Non-superimposable mirror images; need 4 different groups on C
  • Meso: Has stereocentres but achiral (internal plane of symmetry)
  • Racemic: 50:50 enantiomers → optically inactive
Electrophiles & Nucleophiles
  • Electrophiles (E⁺): Electron-deficient (Lewis acids) — H⁺, NO₂⁺, BF₃
  • Nucleophiles (Nu⁻): Electron-rich (Lewis bases) — OH⁻, CN⁻, NH₃, H₂O
  • Strength of Nu: charge > electronegativity; polarizability matters in SN2
Hydrocarbons
Alkanes — CₙH₂ₙ₊₂
  • sp³ carbons; σ bonds only
  • Combustion: CₙH₂ₙ₊₂ + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O
  • Free Radical Halogenation: X₂/hν → alkyl halide (Cl₂ > Br₂ selectivity)
  • Reactivity: F₂ > Cl₂ > Br₂ > I₂
Alkenes — CₙH₂ₙ (Electrophilic Addition)
  • Hydrogenation: H₂/Ni,Pd,Pt → alkane
  • Halogenation: Br₂ → vicinal dibromide (anti addition via bromonium)
  • Hydrohalogenation: HX → Markovnikov product; HBr/peroxide → anti-Markovnikov
  • Hydration: H₂O/H₂SO₄ → Markovnikov alcohol
  • Baeyer's (cold KMnO₄): syn-dihydroxylation → vicinal diol (violet → colourless)
  • Ozonolysis: O₃ then Zn/H₂O → aldehydes/ketones
Alkynes — CₙH₂ₙ₋₂
  • sp carbons; triple bond
  • Hydrogenation: 2H₂/Ni → alkane; Lindlar's → cis-alkene; Na/liq.NH₃ → trans-alkene
  • Hydration: HgSO₄/H₂SO₄/H₂O → ketone (enol → keto); CH≡CH → acetaldehyde
  • Acidity: Terminal alkynes lose H⁺ (sp carbon, pKa ~25); react with NaNH₂
Benzene — Aromatic (EAS)
  • Hückel's rule: Cyclic, planar, conjugated, (4n+2)π electrons
  • Nitration: HNO₃/H₂SO₄ → nitrobenzene
  • Halogenation: X₂/FeX₃ → halobenzene
  • Sulfonation: Conc. H₂SO₄ → benzenesulfonic acid (reversible)
  • F-C Alkylation: RX/AlCl₃ → alkylbenzene (rearrangement + polysubstitution possible)
  • F-C Acylation: RCOCl/AlCl₃ → acylbenzene (no rearrangement, no poly)
  • o/p Directors: Activators (+M,+I) — OH, NH₂, CH₃, halogens (deactivating but o/p)
  • m Directors: Deactivators (−M,−I) — NO₂, COOH, CHO, CN
Haloalkanes & Haloarenes
Preparation of Haloalkanes
  • ROH + HX/ZnCl₂ → RX (Lucas: 3°>2°>1°)
  • ROH + PCl₅ → RCl
  • C=C + HX → Markovnikov product
  • R−H + X₂/hν → free radical halogenation
  • Finkelstein: R−Cl/Br + NaI/acetone → R−I
  • Swarts: R−Br/Cl + AgF → R−F
  • Wurtz: 2RX + 2Na/dry ether → R−R (symmetrical)
  • Wurtz-Fittig: RX + ArX + 2Na → Ar−R
  • Fittig: 2ArX + 2Na → Ar−Ar
Reactions of Haloalkanes
  • SN1 (3°) or SN2 (1°) with nucleophiles
  • Dehydrohalogenation: KOH(alc.) → alkene (Zaitsev, E2)
  • Grignard: RX + Mg/dry ether → RMgX
  • Reduction: RX + Zn/HCl or LiAlH₄ → RH
Haloarenes
  • Unreactive to SN (sp² C, resonance stabilizes C−X, phenyl carbocation unstable)
  • EWG at o/p positions activate ring for nucleophilic substitution
  • Directive effect: halogens are o/p directors (deactivating)
  • Nucleophilic Aromatic Substitution possible only with strong EWG (NO₂) at o/p
Alcohols, Phenols & Ethers
Alcohols (R−OH)
  • Prep: Alkene hydration, carbonyl reduction (NaBH₄, LiAlH₄), Grignard + RCHO
  • H-bonding → high BP; miscible with water (small chain)
  • React with Na → alkoxide + H₂ gas
  • Esterification: RCOOH + R'OH ⇌ RCOOR' (H⁺ cat.)
  • Dehydration: H₂SO₄/heat → alkene
  • Oxidation 1°: PCC → aldehyde; K₂Cr₂O₇ → carboxylic acid
  • Oxidation 2°: CrO₃ → ketone
  • Oxidation 3°: No oxidation under mild conditions
  • Lucas test: ZnCl₂/HCl — 3° turbid immediately, 2° in 5 min, 1° no turbidity
Phenols (Ar−OH)
  • Prep: Cumene process; diazonium salt hydrolysis
  • More acidic than alcohols (phenoxide stabilized by resonance)
  • −OH is o/p activating director (EAS)
  • Nitration: Dilute HNO₃ → o/p nitrophenol
  • Bromination: Br₂(aq) → 2,4,6-tribromophenol (no catalyst needed)
  • FeCl₃ test: Violet/blue/green colour
  • Kolbe's: NaOH + CO₂/140°C → salicylic acid
  • Reimer-Tiemann: CHCl₃/NaOH → salicylaldehyde
Ethers (R−O−R')
  • Williamson synthesis: R'ONa + RX → R'OR (use 1° RX to avoid E2)
  • Dehydration: 2ROH + H₂SO₄/140°C → ROR
  • Cleavage: conc. HX → RX + ROH (or 2RX)
  • Lower BP than alcohols (no H-bonding with itself)
  • Peroxide formation on standing in air (hazard!)
Aldehydes, Ketones & Carboxylic Acids
Preparation
  • Aldehydes: 1° alcohol + PCC; ozonolysis; Rosenmund (RCOCl + H₂/Pd−BaSO₄); DIBAL-H on nitrile
  • Ketones: 2° alcohol + CrO₃; Friedel-Crafts acylation
  • Carboxylic acids: 1° alcohol/aldehyde oxidation; nitrile hydrolysis; Grignard + CO₂
Nucleophilic Addition
  • HCN: → cyanohydrin (↑ C chain by 1)
  • Grignard: HCHO → 1° alc.; RCHO → 2° alc.; ketone → 3° alc.
  • Alcohols: → hemiacetal → acetal/ketal
  • 2,4-DNP: yellow/orange/red ppt (test for C=O)
  • Aldehydes are more reactive than ketones (steric + electronic)
Special Reactions
  • Aldol condensation: compound with α-H + dil. base/acid → β-hydroxy carbonyl
  • Cannizzaro: No α-H + conc. NaOH → disproportionation (alcohol + acid)
  • Haloform: CH₃COR or CH₃CH(OH)R + X₂/NaOH → CHX₃↓ (iodoform yellow)
  • Clemmensen: C=O + Zn-Hg/HCl → CH₂ (alkane)
  • Wolf-Kishner: C=O + NH₂NH₂/KOH/glycol → CH₂ (alkane)
  • Tollens': Aldehydes only → silver mirror
  • Fehling's: Aldehydes only → red Cu₂O ppt
Carboxylic Acids
  • Acidic (carboxylate resonance); blue litmus → red; NaHCO₃ → effervescence
  • Esterification: RCOOH + R'OH ⇌ RCOOR' (Fischer, H⁺ cat.)
  • Reduction: LiAlH₄ → 1° alcohol (not NaBH₄)
  • HVZ: R−CH₂−COOH + X₂/Red P → α-halo acid
  • Reactivity of derivatives: Acid halide > Anhydride > Ester > Amide
Organic Nitrogen Compounds
Amines — Preparation
  • Nitro reduction: ArNO₂ + Sn/HCl or Fe/HCl → ArNH₂
  • Hofmann ammonolysis: RX + excess NH₃ → mixture of 1°, 2°, 3° amines + quat. salt
  • Gabriel phthalimide: phthalimide + KOH + RX + H₂O/H⁺ → only 1° amine
  • Hofmann bromamide: RCONH₂ + Br₂/KOH → RNH₂ (one less carbon)
  • Reduction of nitriles: RCN + LiAlH₄ → RCH₂NH₂
Amine Reactions
  • Acylation: R−NH₂ + R'COCl → R−NHCOR' (amide)
  • Carbylamine (1° only): RNH₂ + CHCl₃/KOH → RNC (foul smell — test for 1°)
  • Diazotization: ArNH₂ + NaNO₂/HCl, 0−5°C → ArN₂⁺Cl⁻
  • Sandmeyer: ArN₂⁺Cl⁻ + CuX/HX → Ar−X; + CuCN → Ar−CN
  • Gattermann: Same as Sandmeyer but Cu powder (less clean)
  • Coupling: ArN₂⁺ + phenol/aniline → azo dye (orange/red)
Cyanides & Isocyanides
  • RCN prep: RX + KCN (SN2)
  • RNC prep: RX + AgCN → isocyanide
  • RCN hydrolysis: → RCOOH (via amide)
  • RCN reduction: LiAlH₄ → 1° amine (RCH₂NH₂)
  • DIBAL-H on RCN: → aldehyde
Biomolecules, Polymers & Everyday Chemistry
Carbohydrates
  • Mono: Glucose (aldohexose), Fructose (ketohexose)
  • Di: Sucrose (Glc+Fru, non-reducing), Lactose (Glc+Gal, reducing), Maltose (Glc+Glc, reducing)
  • Poly: Starch, Cellulose (β-1,4), Glycogen (animal starch)
  • Reducing sugars: all monosaccharides, lactose, maltose (not sucrose)
  • Test: Tollens' (silver mirror), Fehling's (red ppt)
Proteins & Nucleic Acids
  • Polymers of α-amino acids linked by peptide bonds (−CO−NH−)
  • 1° = sequence; 2° = α-helix/β-sheet; 3° = 3D; 4° = multi-chain
  • Denaturation: loss of 3D structure (heat, pH, heavy metals)
  • DNA: deoxyribose, A-T (2H bonds), G-C (3H bonds), double helix
  • RNA: ribose, U replaces T, single strand
Polymers
  • Addition: No byproduct — PE, PVC, Teflon, Polypropylene, Polyacrylonitrile
  • Condensation: Eliminate small molecule — Polyester, Nylon-6,6, Nylon-6, Bakelite
  • Nylon-6,6: hexamethylenediamine + adipic acid
  • Nylon-6: Caprolactam (ring-opening polymerisation)
  • Bakelite: phenol + formaldehyde (cross-linked, thermosetting)
Chemistry in Everyday Life
  • Analgesics/Antipyretics: Aspirin, Paracetamol
  • Antiseptics: Dettol (living tissue)
  • Disinfectants: Phenol (inanimate surfaces)
  • Antibiotics: Penicillin (bactericidal)
  • Soaps: Fatty acid salts; form scum with hard water
  • Detergents: Synthetic; no scum with hard water

Common Traps & Exceptions ⚠️
Anti-Markovnikov — HBr only

Anti-Markovnikov addition occurs only with HBr in the presence of peroxides (free radical mechanism). HCl and HI do not give anti-Markovnikov even with peroxides.

Friedel-Crafts Alkylation Traps

Carbocation rearrangement is possible (forms more stable carbocation). Polysubstitution is common. Does not work with deactivated rings (NO₂, COOH groups present).

Haloarenes — Unreactive to SN

ArX does not undergo SN reactions under normal conditions because the C−X bond has partial double bond character (resonance). Nucleophilic aromatic substitution (SNAr) requires strong EWG at o/p positions.

Williamson Synthesis — Use 1° Halide

Always use 1° alkyl halide to avoid competing E2 elimination. With 2° or 3° halides, elimination is favoured over substitution.

Gabriel Phthalimide — Only 1° Amines

Gabriel synthesis works only for 1° amines. 2° or 3° amines cannot be made by this method.

Hofmann Bromamide — Loss of Carbon

Product has one less carbon than starting amide. RCONH₂ → RNH₂ (e.g., propanamide → ethylamine).

Diazotization — Temperature Critical

Must be done at 0−5°C. Diazonium salt decomposes above this. Works only for aromatic 1° amines. Aliphatic amines give unstable diazonium salts.

Basicity of Amines — Gas vs Aqueous

Gas phase: 3° > 2° > 1° > NH₃ (inductive effect only). Aqueous phase (methylamines): 2° > 1° > 3° > NH₃ (solvation reverses order for 3°).

Sucrose is Non-Reducing

Sucrose does NOT give Tollens' or Fehling's test positive because the anomeric C of both glucose and fructose are involved in the glycosidic bond. All other common disaccharides (maltose, lactose) are reducing.

NaBH₄ vs LiAlH₄

NaBH₄ reduces only aldehydes/ketones. LiAlH₄ reduces carboxylic acids, esters, amides too. LiAlH₄ cannot be used in protic solvents (reacts violently with water/alcohol).

Haloform Reaction Scope

Iodoform test positive for: CH₃COR, CH₃CHO (acetaldehyde), and secondary alcohols CH₃CH(OH)R. Not for other aldehydes or ketones.
